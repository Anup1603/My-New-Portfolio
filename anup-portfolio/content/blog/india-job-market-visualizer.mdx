India has about **63.7 crore workers** doing wildly different things — farming, delivery, retail, software, care work. This map puts them all on one screen: sized by how many people do each job, and coloured by how much today's AI can actually touch them.

## How to read it

- **Each tile is one job** — cultivators, construction labourers, software developers, insurance agents.
- **Bigger tile = more people** do that job. Farming is huge; software is tiny.
- **Colour = the layer you pick** — AI exposure, pay, education, growth, or sector.

Tap any tile for its details, and use the buttons above the map to switch layers.

## The big finding: India runs "cool"

- Weighted by how many people do each job, India's average AI exposure is **~2.9 out of 10**. The US map this is based on sits at **~4.9**.
- Only **~5%** of Indian workers are in high-exposure (7+) jobs — data entry, accounts, call centres, bank clerks. In the US, it's **~42%**.
- **Two-thirds** are in low-exposure, hands-on or informal work.

This isn't about India being "behind." It's a different labour structure — most people work with their hands or face-to-face, out of a chatbot's reach.

## What "AI exposure" means

A **0–10 estimate** of how much of a job today's AI could do or speed up. Desk-and-screen work scores high; physical or in-person work — farming, nursing, driving, repairs — scores low.

One caveat worth keeping in mind: a high score means a job will likely **change, not vanish**. Bank clerks lived through exactly this with core banking and UPI — the work changed shape; the people didn't disappear.

## Why India mirrors the US

- **Farming** employs ~**24 crore** people — about the population of Pakistan. It sets the temperature of the whole map.
- **IT** employs ~**30 lakh** developers — huge in rupees and exports, but a small tile in headcount.
- **~84%** of non-farm work is informal — no contract, little digital surface for AI to grab onto.

Where the US concentrates people at screens, India concentrates them in fields, on roads, in kirana stores, and in people's homes.

## The six layers

- **AI exposure** — what AI can do or speed up today.
- **Future resilience** — which jobs look steady over the next 5–10 years.
- **Median pay** — often, the best-paid office jobs are the most exposed.
- **Education** — the schooling people in the job usually have.
- **Growth outlook** — whether the job is expanding or shrinking.
- **Sector** — agriculture, IT, healthcare, transport, and so on.

## "Future resilience", in one formula

Exposure alone doesn't tell you if a job is a good place to be — farm labour is safe from AI but shrinking and low-paid. So resilience combines two things: how shielded a job is, and whether demand is growing.

```js
// resilience = 60% "how shielded from AI" + 40% "demand trend"
function resilience(job) {
  const aiShielding = 10 - job.ai; // low exposure -> high shielding
  const demandTrend = GROWTH_SCORE[job.growth]; // e.g. declining=2, growing=8
  return 0.6 * aiShielding + 0.4 * demandTrend;
}
```

It's a lens for thinking, not a forecast.

## One honest caveat

The per-tile numbers are **careful estimates, not official statistics**. What's anchored to real data is the **proportions** — tile sizes match government survey shares (~43% of workers in farming, ~84% of non-farm work informal, ~63.7 crore total). So trust the _shape_ of the map, not any single tile's number.

## The takeaway

India's AI conversation is usually imported from richer countries where most people already work at screens. India's reality is different — most of the country works with its hands or with other people, and most of that work stays cool on the AI map, for now.

Open the map, pick a layer, and click around. The proportions tend to surprise people — which is rather the point.
